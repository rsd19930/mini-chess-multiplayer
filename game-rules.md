Pico Chess - Official Game Rules
This is a short and simpler variant of the original chess. This variant is more fun and simple for people to understand.
1. Game Objective:
The primary objective is to checkmate the opponent's King.
2. Board and Pieces:
Board: The game is played on a 6x6 checkered board. Squares are identified using algebraic notation (e.g., a1, f6).
Pieces: Each player starts with the following set of pieces:
* 1 King (K)
* 1 Rook (R)
* 1 Knight (N)
* 1 Bishop (B)
* 1 Pawn (P)
3. Starting Setup:
The board is set up as follows (assuming a1 is the bottom-left square for White):
* White:
King (K): a1
Rook (R): b1
Knight (N): c1
Bishop (B): d1
Pawn (P): a2
* Black:
King (K): f6
Rook (R): e6
Knight (N): d6
Bishop (B): c6
Pawn (P): f5
White moves first.
4. Gameplay and Turns:
Players alternate making one move per turn.
On a player's turn, they must perform one of the following actions:
* Move a Piece: Move one of their pieces already on the board to a new square according to its movement rules.
* Drop a Piece: Place a piece from their "hand" (pieces captured from the opponent) onto any empty square on the board.
If a player fails to make a legal move within their allotted turn time, their turn is skipped, and play passes to the opponent (see Rule 10).
5. Piece Movement:
General: Standard chess movement rules apply to all pieces unless specified otherwise below.
King (K): Moves one square in any direction (horizontally, vertically, or diagonally) to an unoccupied square or a square occupied by an opponent's piece (which it then captures). The King cannot move into check.
Rook (R): Moves any number of unoccupied squares horizontally or vertically.
Knight (N): Moves in an "L" shape: two squares in one cardinal direction (horizontal or vertical) and then one square at a 90-degree angle. The Knight can jump over other pieces.
Bishop (B): Moves any number of unoccupied squares diagonally. (Note: Both players start with a light-squared Bishop).
Pawn (P):
* Forward Movement: Pawns move one square forward (towards the opponent's side of the board) to an unoccupied square.
* No Initial Two-Square Move: Pawns do not have an option to move two squares forward on their first move.
* Capturing: Pawns capture by moving one square diagonally forward to a square occupied by an opponent's piece.
Special Moves Not Applicable:
* Castling: There is no castling in Pico Chess.
* En Passant: There is no en passant capture in Pico Chess (as pawns do not have an initial two-square move).
6. Pawn Promotion:
If a pawn reaches the opponent's back rank (rank 6 for White's pawns, rank 1 for Black's pawns), it must be immediately promoted as part of the same move.
The player whose pawn is being promoted chooses to replace the pawn with either a Rook, Knight, or Bishop of their own color.
The chosen piece replaces the pawn on the promotion square. The effect of the new piece is immediate (e.g., it can deliver check or checkmate).
7. Capturing and Drops (Crazyhouse Mechanic):
Capturing: When a piece makes a legal move to a square occupied by an opponent's piece, the opponent's piece is captured and removed from the board.
Adding to Hand: The captured piece is added to the capturing player's "hand" (or "reserve"). The piece changes color to that of the capturing player. (e.g., If White captures a Black Rook, White now has a White Rook in their hand, available to be dropped).
Dropping:
* On their turn, instead of moving a piece on the board, a player may choose to "drop" a piece from their hand onto any empty square on the board. This constitutes their entire move for that turn.
* Dropped pieces (King, Rook, Knight, Bishop, Pawn) can deliver check or checkmate immediately upon being placed, provided the placement is legal.
Pawn Drop Restrictions:
* A pawn cannot be dropped onto the promotion rank (rank 6 for White, rank 1 for Black).
* A pawn can be dropped on any other empty square, including its starting rank or any rank before the promotion rank. Once dropped, it moves and captures like a normal pawn.
8. Winning and Losing the Game:
A player wins the game, and their opponent loses, under the following conditions:
Checkmate: The opponent's King is checkmated. This occurs when the King is in check and there is no legal move (including moving the King, blocking the check, or capturing the checking piece/dropping a piece to block) to remove the King from check.
Resignation: The opponent resigns.
Game Abandonment: If a player leaves or exits the game while it is in progress, they forfeit the game, and the remaining player is declared the winner.
Stalemate (Loss for Stalemated Player): If the player whose turn it is has no legal moves, and their King is not in check, that player loses the game.
Turn Skipped while in Check: If a player’s King is in Check state and the player fails to make a legal move within the turn timer (see time control section) the opponent player wins. 
9. Draw Conditions:
The game is a draw only under the following condition:
Agreement: Both players mutually agree to a draw.
10. Time Control:
* Each player has 30 seconds to make their move for each turn.
* A player's 30-second timer begins as soon as their opponent completes their move.
* If a player's timer reaches zero before they complete a legal move, their turn is immediately skipped, and play passes to the opponent. The game does not end due to this; the game continues until a win/loss/draw condition from Rule 8 or 9 is met.
